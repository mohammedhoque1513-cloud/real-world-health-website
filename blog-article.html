Why evidence-led strategy still fails without implementation realism

10 Apr 2026StrategyAlex Morgan

Strong recommendations are not enough. Health strategies fail when they ignore ownership, behaviour, operational friction, and the realities of day-to-day delivery.

The gap between recommendation and reality

Many health strategies are analytically strong yet operationally fragile. They describe the right destination, but they do not fully engage with how the organisation will move. The result is familiar: ambitious decks, enthusiastic steering groups, and limited movement once the work hits the real world.

In practice, the problem is rarely that leaders do not understand the evidence. More often, the issue is that recommendations are not translated into workable accountabilities, credible sequencing, and delivery choices that respect the context teams are operating within.

The most useful strategy documents are not the smartest. They are the ones teams can actually act on.

What implementation realism looks like

Implementation realism starts with curiosity about constraints. Which teams will own the work? What behaviour needs to change? Which decisions require executive sponsorship? Where will operational friction show up first? The earlier these questions are surfaced, the more resilient the strategy becomes.

Realism does not mean lowering ambition. It means being precise about the path between intent and execution. It asks whether governance is credible, whether measurement is proportionate, and whether frontline users can absorb the change being proposed.

Practical Advice

If a recommendation cannot be assigned, sequenced, measured, and explained in plain language, it is probably not ready yet.

Questions senior teams should ask

  • What assumptions are we making about capacity, incentives, and leadership attention?
  • Which parts of the strategy are genuinely ready for implementation, and which remain exploratory?
  • How will we know whether adoption is happening in practice, not just on paper?
  • Are we asking delivery teams to absorb too much change at once?

The organisations that move well tend to treat implementation design as part of strategy, not a downstream activity. They structure the work so evidence, ownership, and delivery considerations evolve together. That usually leads to fewer surprises, clearer leadership decisions, and greater credibility with the teams responsible for making change stick.

Article Summary

Strategy fails when delivery assumptions are invisible. Make ownership, sequencing, measurement, and behavioural realities explicit early.
